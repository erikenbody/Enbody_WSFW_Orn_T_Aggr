We analyzed circulating T from 30 adult females and 26 adult males in the M.a.lorentzi population with unornamented females and 84 adult females and 95 adult males in the M.a.moretoni population with ornamented females. A greater proportion of ornamented female samples had detectable levels of T (0.5) than did unornamented female samples (0.3), but this effect was not significant (glm: z= 1.865, df=112, p=0.062). Probability of T detection also did not differ between males of each population (moretoni: 0.8 vs. lorentzi: 0.96 ; glm: z= -1.743, df=119, p=0.081).

The top model for the effect of different predictors on circulating T levels included the interaction between subspecies and sex, the interaction between subspecies and year, and julian date (Table 1; we report AIC values for stepwise model comparisons in Table S1). Circulating T was significantly predicted by the interaction between subspecies and sex and also year (Table 1). Between sex and between population comparisons (following a Tukeys adjustment for multiple comparisons) of circulating T levels indicate that ornamented females had higher levels of T than did unornamented females and females of both populations were lower in circulating T than males (Table 2). Males differed in the opposite direction; males of M.a.lorentzi, where females are unornamented, had higher levels of circulating T than did males in M.a.moretoni, where females are ornamented (Table 2).
